## Keeping the human in the loop

*(5 minutes)*

This video addresses two of the most common concerns professionals have about organizational AI adoption: dependency and preserving human connection.

#### Key takeaways

* **Being "the human in the loop" means more than oversight** — you decide what problems AI should solve.
* **Avoid dependency through understanding, not avoidance:** can you explain what the AI is doing? If yes, that's a good sign.
* **AI should free you for more human work, not less** — the handwritten card, the personalized outreach, the relationship building.
* **Set cultural norms around productivity early:** discuss what happens with the time AI saves.
* **An AI policy helps scale your understanding** — documenting decisions about platforms, task delegation, oversight, transparency, and values.

## Exercise

### Exercise: Draft your AI use policy

*You've now used AI across research, data, and end-to-end workflows. This exercise uses everything you've built in the course — starting with your context document — to write a short, honest AI policy for your business.*

**Part I: Reflect** *(in your own notes, 5–7 minutes)*

Pull up your context document from Lesson 1. Read it with fresh eyes — you've done several exercises since you wrote it. Then answer these three questions:

1. **What tasks am I now confident handing to AI?** Be specific — not "writing" but "drafting first-pass responses to common customer inquiries." 2. **What will I always keep human?** Name the work where your judgment, relationships, or accountability are non-negotiable. 3. **What do my customers and team need to know about how I use AI?** Consider what you'd say if someone asked directly.

**Part II: Build it with AI**

This policy will serve as a shared reference for you and anyone on your team — a clear agreement about how AI gets used in your business, so everyone is working from the same understanding rather than making it up as they go.

Share your context document and your answers from Part I with AI and ask it to help you draft a one-page AI use policy with three sections:

* **What we use AI for** — specific approved tasks and tools
* **What stays human** — the work AI doesn't touch and why
* **How we stay accountable** — oversight, transparency, and what happens when something goes wrong

Then do what you'd do with any AI output: read it, fix what doesn't sound like your business, add what's missing, cut what's not true.

**Part III: Stress-test it**

Read your finished policy and ask yourself: *Is there anything I softened that I should say more directly?* If a customer asked "do you use AI in your business?" — does this policy give you a clear, honest answer to share?

Hi there. My name is Corey, and I work at Prospect Butcher. Throughout this course, you've practiced using the 4D framework for customer communication, market research, and business strategy. In this final video, we're going to zoom out and address two questions that are top of mind for many AI users. One, how do I make sure I don't become too dependent on AI? And two, how do I balance efficiency with thoughtfulness? Both questions come down to the same thing, being the human in the loop. You've probably heard the phrase human in the loop many times before. But with AI, it means something very specific. You're the one who decides what problems AI should solve. You evaluate whether its outputs meet the standards of your organization and profession, and you maintain the domain expertise, the experimental reasoning, and the human thinking that AI simply cannot replicate. That sounds straightforward, but in practice, it gets very complicated. So, let's go ahead and talk about what this looks like today. Here's what makes AI different from software tools you may use today. It is not deterministic. The same input will produce a slightly different response every single time. And that means you can't just validate it once and move on. Here's a good check. Can you explain what the AI is doing and why you're using it for this task? If yes, that's healthy augmentation. If you find yourself accepting outputs without scrutiny because AI is usually right, that's a signal to step back. Say you're using AI to draft a report. Healthy augmentation looks like reviewing the draft against your thesis, checking the arguments reflect your sound reasoning, and verifying facts come from valid sources. Overdependence looks like sending the draft forward because it reads well and the formatting is clean. The efficiency question is much trickier. AI can absolutely help you cut through administrative tasks and repetitive work so you can focus on work that requires your deepest expertise, interpreting a complex data set. But there's a risk. When AI speeds things up, expectations often speed up, too. And that pressure can erode the careful thinking that defines great work. Let's consider a small business owner using AI to draft customer proposals. The drafts come back fast and they're well organized, and they save an hour of work per quote. But if she starts sending them without checking the pricing and scope because the drafts just look right, she's traded accuracy for speed. The human in the loop isn't just reviewing AI's output. They're maintaining the standard of evidence that other groups depend on. As you bring AI into your teams and workflows, an organizational AI policy helps you scale these principles beyond individual judgment calls. And here's a few areas worth addressing early. Platform awareness. Which AI tools are approved? Task boundaries. What work gets AI assistance? What stays fully human? How do you handle these gray areas? Quality and oversight. Who reviews AI outputs before they enter regulated workflows? What validation is required? Transparency. How do you communicate AI use to regulators, customers, and partners? What are the disclosure requirements in your context? Time saved. How do you redirect the time AI frees up? Setting realistic expectations very early on prevents the do more with less trap. Your training, your domain expertise, your understanding of systems and practical realities, your relationships with collaborators and customers, all of this shapes how you approach challenges in ways that are completely your own. AI can help you leverage those qualities more effectively. It can help you learn faster, communicate more clearly, and analyze data more efficiently. But it can't replace the judgement and the care that you bring to running your business. That's what AI fluency means. And that's the work only you can do.
